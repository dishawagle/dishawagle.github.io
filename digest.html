Stage 01 Problem definition
▼

"Product managers at tech companies have abandoned any consistent habit of tracking AI research and news — not because they lack interest, but because no existing solution filters and frames information for their specific role."

Problem statement · derived from discovery · April 2026
Who

Primary: Product managers at tech companies working on or adjacent to AI products. They understand why AI matters. They've tried existing solutions and found them either too shallow, too broad, or too dense. So they've stopped trying altogether.

Beachhead segment

PMs actively job-hunting or recently placed in AI PM roles — highest motivation, highest willingness to adopt, most likely to evangelize. Natural wedge before expanding to employed PMs.

The cost

They miss product opportunities and pivots they should be proposing. They waste the limited reading time they have on content that doesn't connect to their daily decisions. The anxiety is chronic, not situational.

No consistent habit

The user isn't optimising an existing routine — they've given up entirely. The product isn't competing with newsletters. It's filling a vacuum.

Willing but unsupported

When asked "would you read it immediately?" — the answer is yes. The user is not the problem. The available solutions are. This is a product gap, not a behaviour change problem.

Opportunity framing

The anxiety is about missing opportunities, not lacking knowledge. Value prop is "never miss what matters to you" — not "understand AI better."

Hypotheses to test in user research
H1 — Relevance over volume

PMs will engage consistently if content is pre-filtered to their role — even if it covers fewer topics than existing newsletters.

Test: show 5 role-specific takeaways vs 20 generic headlines. Measure preference and recall.
H2 — Monday morning delivery

Monday morning is the highest-value delivery window because PMs are preparing for the week ahead, not winding down.

Test: ask interviewees when they're most receptive to new information and describe their Monday routine.
H3 — Opportunity framing over knowledge framing

The anxiety is about missing opportunities, not lacking knowledge — meaning the core value prop is "never miss a relevant development" not "understand AI better."

Test: ask interviewees to describe the last time they felt behind, and what the actual consequence was.

Key findings
01 The problem is translation, not access — and it's universal

All three participants — regardless of how much they read — hit the same wall: they cannot reliably extract what an AI development means for their specific product, company, and domain.

Participant A: "I understood the words but I couldn't connect it to what it meant for our product decisions."
Participant B: "I'm always doing this mental translation — does this matter for my domain specifically? Sometimes I genuinely don't know."
Participant C: "There's no layer that connects what I've read to what I might actually need it for."
02 Every existing solution fails at the same layer

Participants have tried newsletters, X, ChatGPT, peer networks, and note systems. All fail to provide domain-specific, role-specific "so what." They solve discovery but not relevance.

Participant A: "It's written for researchers and engineers — I have to do all the translation work myself."
Participant B: "ChatGPT tells me what the paper says. It won't tell me whether it matters for my roadmap."
Participant C: Tried RSS feeds, 40 follows on X, 3 Discord servers — still making gut calls under pressure.
03 The emotional cost is higher than it appears

None of the three experience this as a mild inconvenience. Two participants admitted to performing confidence they don't fully feel. The anxiety is persistent, not situational.

Participant A: "There's this background anxiety that never fully goes away — what if the thing you missed is the thing that matters?"
Participant B: "I've gotten quite good at performing confidence I don't fully feel. It works. But it's exhausting."
04 The problem extends beyond the individual — it's a team alignment gap

Participant C surfaced a dimension the others didn't name: entire product teams operate from fragmented, unshared mental models of where AI is heading — and make decisions at the intersection of those fragments.

Participant C: "We're good at responding to explosions. We're bad at tracking the slow burn."

Strategic implications
Implication 01
Don't compete on volume or speed
TLDR AI and The Rundown win on scale and daily cadence. The product wins by being the only one that asks "what's your role?" — depth and relevance over breadth and recency.
Implication 02
Weekly cadence is a feature, not a limitation
Daily newsletters contribute to the overload problem. A weekly digest that covers only what matters signals respect for the user's time. "5 things, once a week, none of which you can safely ignore."
Implication 03 · Moat
Role + domain personalisation is the defensible layer
Any newsletter can add an AI summary. The defensible advantage is a product that learns the user's role, domain, company stage, and product context over time — making takeaways progressively more accurate and harder to replicate.

Not more content. Not faster headlines. A filter with judgment that gets smarter the longer you use it.

Eliminate the translation gap between what AI research produces and what product managers can act on — one role, one domain, one decision at a time.

Weekly one-question survey: "Did anything in this week's digest influence a work decision?" Target: 40%+ of WAUs answering yes within 60 days of launch.

Product design principles
01
Respect the time budget
Every issue must be completable in under 5 minutes. If it takes longer, we've failed at curation.
02
Judgment over coverage
5 things done well beats 20 things adequately. Telling users what to skip is as valuable as surfacing what matters.
03
Replace habits, don't add to them
The product must displace existing behaviour — not add a new obligation to an already overloaded schedule.
04
Context compounds
Every interaction should make the product smarter about the user. Value at month 6 should visibly exceed value at month 1.
05
Earn the upgrade
The free tier must be genuinely valuable. Users upgrade because they want more, not because the free version failed them.
06
Never manufacture urgency
No "you missed 47 AI developments" anxiety loops. The product's job is to reduce anxiety, not exploit it.

Key decisions log
Why email + web app rather than web app only?
Email creates the habit passively — it arrives on Monday without the user having to remember to visit a URL. The web app provides the full experience but cannot be the primary v1 surface because it requires building a visit habit from scratch. Email is the push, web is the pull.
Why hybrid curation rather than full automation?
Trust is the product. One issue with a low-quality item breaks the habit we've spent weeks building. Human approval costs 30 minutes per week in v1 and is worth every minute. Automate editorial judgment once we have enough issues to train against.
Why no domain filter in v1?
If we ship role + domain simultaneously and the product doesn't work, we won't know whether the problem was the role framing, the domain framing, or both. Ship role-only, prove the translation model works, then add domain as the first v2 upgrade.

What was built
engine.py — the digest pipeline

Fetches from 9 sources (OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Hugging Face, Google Research, Microsoft Research, AWS AI via RSS; Anthropic, Meta AI, Mistral via ArXiv), deduplicates across runs using MD5 fingerprints, selects the 5 most PM-relevant items using Gemini, and generates role-specific takeaways with Act / Watch / Skip signals.

review.py — internal editorial dashboard

Streamlit-based human approval UI. Each draft item is editable — title, summary, PM takeaway, relevance signal. Nothing goes live until all 5 items are approved. Approved items are logged to prevent republishing in future issues.

app.py — public web reader

Self-contained Streamlit app deployed on Streamlit Community Cloud. Fetches and generates live on first visit, caches for 7 days. Features: Act/Watch/Skip relevance signals, PM takeaways, per-item Q&A powered by Gemini (with suggested starter questions and conversation history), weekly pulse survey measuring decision influence rate, and a subscribe form.

Stack

Key decisions made during build
Switched from Anthropic API to Gemini — free tier enabled zero-cost build
The original spec used the Claude API, but Anthropic requires paid credits. Gemini 2.0 Flash has a generous free tier, making the entire product buildable and deployable at zero cost — important for a portfolio project that needs to stay live indefinitely.
Self-contained app over JSON file dependency
The original architecture stored approved digests as local JSON files, which don't exist on Streamlit Cloud. Redesigned app.py to fetch and generate live on first load, cached for 7 days — making the app fully self-contained with no file dependencies.
Hybrid curation maintained as a product principle
The public app generates automatically, but the local workflow (engine.py + review.py) preserves human approval for issues distributed to subscribers. Fully automated output is the public demo; curated output is the real product. Trust is built by the human review layer, not despite its absence.
